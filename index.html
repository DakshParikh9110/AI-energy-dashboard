The Netflix comparison

Streaming one hour of Netflix on a laptop uses approximately 8 watt-hours. At 0.4 Wh per AI prompt, that is equivalent in energy terms to around 20 individual prompts.

The 10x myth

The widely cited claim that ChatGPT uses 10x more energy than a Google search originates from a 2023 study using early GPT-3 hardware. The 2025 measured gap is approximately 40%.

Where did the 10x figure come from? ›

A 2023 estimate applied to 2025 hardware

In 2023, researcher Alex de Vries estimated that a ChatGPT query used approximately 3 watt-hours, based on early GPT-3 hardware running at low GPU utilisation. Goldman Sachs cited this figure widely. It was a reasonable estimate for that hardware at that time.

By 2025, GPT-4o runs on significantly more efficient infrastructure. Epoch AI measured actual inference at approximately 0.3 to 0.4 watt-hours per prompt. A Google search uses approximately 0.3 watt-hours. The gap is real but modest. The 10x figure is still widely repeated in media coverage.

The efficiency paradox

GPT-4o uses approximately 10x less energy per prompt than 2023 estimates. ChatGPT grew from 300 million to 800 million weekly active users between December 2024 and April 2025. Total energy demand rose.

Why does this keep happening? ›

When things get cheaper, people use more of them

In 1865, economist William Stanley Jevons noticed something counterintuitive: making steam engines more efficient did not reduce Britain's coal consumption. It increased it. Cheaper, more efficient engines spread to more industries, more factories, more applications. Total consumption went up.

The same pattern appeared in cars, refrigerators, air conditioning and LED lighting. Each time, efficiency made the technology more accessible, which expanded usage faster than the efficiency gains could offset.

AI is following the same path. GPT-4o costs roughly 10x less energy per prompt than early GPT-3 models. AI became accessible to hundreds of millions more people. Total energy demand from AI data centres rose sharply. Whether this continues depends on whether renewable energy can grow faster than usage does. That question is not yet answered.

SECTION 04

Three paths: IEA scenario analysis

The IEA publishes three sensitivity cases: High Efficiency, Base Case, and Lift-Off. No probability weights are assigned. These are frameworks for thinking about the range of outcomes, not forecasts.

Click each scenario to expand

High Efficiency

Efficiency gains outpace demand growth

IEA High Efficiency scenario

+

Algorithmic improvements and hardware gains reduce energy per AI operation faster than query volume grows. Models like DeepSeek demonstrate that comparable performance is achievable at significantly lower compute cost. Renewables cover the majority of incremental data centre demand.

Energy Demand

Data centre electricity growth slows relative to the base case. The 945 TWh 2030 projection is not reached.

Climate Progress

Data centre CO2 emissions peak earlier. Tech sector net-zero pledges become more achievable.

Infrastructure Readiness

Reduced pressure on the grid. The 20% project delay risk identified by IEA diminishes.

What Needs to Happen

Sustained algorithmic efficiency gains and renewable build-out proceeding faster than current grid constraints allow.

Base Case

Demand and clean energy grow in parallel

IEA Base Case scenario

+

Current industry forecasts and regulatory conditions persist. Renewables meet approximately half of additional demand growth. Natural gas fills the remainder. Data centres reach approximately 945 TWh by 2030. CO2 emissions peak at around 320 million tonnes by 2030 before a shallow decline as nuclear and renewables displace gas.

Energy Demand

Data centre electricity doubles from 2024 to 2030. Natural gas remains a significant bridging fuel. First small modular reactors come online around 2030.

Climate Progress

Data centres reach approximately 1% of global CO2 emissions by 2030. One of the few sectors where emissions continue to grow.

Infrastructure Readiness

Significant investment required. Around 20% of planned data centre projects face delay risk.

What Needs to Happen

Energy sector investment keeps pace with demand. Grid lead times of 4 to 8 years do not create critical bottlenecks.

Lift-Off

Demand accelerates beyond current projections

IEA Lift-Off scenario

+

AI adoption expands faster than current forecasts. Sovereign AI infrastructure buildouts in Asia and the Middle East add demand beyond hyperscaler projections. Data centre electricity consumption exceeds the base case materially. CO2 emissions from data centres reach approximately 500 million tonnes by 2035.

Energy Demand

Data centre electricity exceeds 945 TWh by 2030. Grid constraints force extended reliance on gas and coal.

Climate Progress

Data centre emissions reach approximately 1.4% of global CO2 by 2030 per IEA estimates.

Infrastructure Readiness

Energy costs rise for residential and industrial consumers as data centre demand competes for grid capacity.

What Needs to Happen

Demand growth significantly exceeds current forecasts AND renewable and grid build-out falls short of current investment commitments.

Redirecting the discussion...

The debate about AI's energy footprint is usually framed as doom or dismissal. The data supports neither. Per-prompt efficiency is genuinely improving. Renewable investment is genuinely accelerating. Demand is also genuinely growing faster than the grid was designed to absorb.

The variable that resolves this is not model efficiency. That has already improved 10x and demand rose anyway. It is grid infrastructure: how fast transmission lines get built, how quickly renewable capacity connects, and whether policy keeps pace with the buildout that private capital is already funding.

That is the story worth watching. Not the energy cost of your next prompt.
